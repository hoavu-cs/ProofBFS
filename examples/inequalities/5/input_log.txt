[Proposer] statement:
For positive real numbers $a,b,c$ satisfying $(a+b-c)(1/a+1/b-1/c)=3$, we have $a/b + b/a > 4$.

proof:
Expand the given equation:
\begin{align*}
(a+b-c)\left(\frac1a+\frac1b-\frac1c\right) &= \frac{a+b-c}{a} + \frac{a+b-c}{b} - \frac{a+b-c}{c} \\
&= \left(1+\frac{b}{a}-\frac{c}{a}\right) + \left(\frac{a}{b}+1-\frac{c}{b}\right) - \left(\frac{a}{c}+\frac{b}{c}-1\right) \\
&= 3 + \left(\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a}\right) - \left(\frac{a}{c}+\frac{c}{a}\right) - \left(\frac{b}{c}+\frac{c}{b}\right).
\end{align*}
Setting this equal to $3$ gives
\[
\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a} = \left(\frac{a}{c}+\frac{c}{a}\right) + \left(\frac{b}{c}+\frac{c}{b}\right).
\]
For any positive $x,y$, the AM–GM inequality yields $x/y + y/x \ge 2$, with equality iff $x=y$. Applying this to the two terms on the right‑hand side we obtain
\[
\frac{a}{c}+\frac{c}{a} \ge 2, \qquad \frac{b}{c}+\frac{c}{b} \ge 2.
\]
Hence
\[
\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a} \ge 2+2 = 4.
\]
If equality held, we would have $a/c = c/a = 1$ and $b/c = c/b = 1$, implying $a=c$ and $b=c$, so $a=b=c$. Substituting $a=b=c$ into the original condition gives $(a+a-a)(1/a+1/a-1/a)=1$, contradicting the hypothesis that the product equals $3$. Therefore the inequality is strict:
\[
\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a} > 4.
\]

[Proposer] statement:
For positive real numbers $a,b,c$ satisfying $(a+b-c)\bigl(\frac1a+\frac1b-\frac1c\bigr)=3$, we have
\[
\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a}\ge 2+2\sqrt3 .
\]
Equality holds if and only if $(a+b)^2=(4+2\sqrt3)\,ab$ and $c=\dfrac{a^2+b^2}{2(a+b)}$.

proof:
Rewrite the condition as
\[
(a+b-c)\Bigl(\frac{a+b}{ab}-\frac1c\Bigr)=3.
\]
Multiplying out and clearing denominators yields the quadratic in $c$:
\[
(a+b)c^{2}-\bigl((a+b)^{2}-2ab\bigr)c+(a+b)ab=0\tag{1}
\]
For a positive real solution $c$ to exist, the discriminant of (1) must be non‑negative:
\[
\Delta:=\bigl((a+b)^{2}-2ab\bigr)^{2}-4(a+b)\cdot(a+b)ab\ge0.
\]
Simplifying,
\[
\Delta=(a+b)^{4}-8(a+b)^{2}ab+4(ab)^{2}\ge0.
\]
Divide by $(ab)^{2}>0$ and set $u:=\dfrac{(a+b)^{2}}{ab}$. Since $\dfrac{(a+b)^{2}}{ab}= \dfrac{a}{b}+\dfrac{b}{a}+2$, we obtain
\[
u^{2}-8u+4\ge0.
\]
The quadratic $u^{2}-8u+4$ has roots $4\pm2\sqrt3$; hence the inequality forces $u\le4-2\sqrt3$ or $u\ge4+2\sqrt3$. From the previously established statement $\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a}>4$ we know $u>4$, therefore $u\ge4+2\sqrt3$. Consequently
\[
\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a}=u-2\ge2+2\sqrt3 .
\]
Equality $\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a}=2+2\sqrt3$ occurs exactly when $\Delta=0$, i.e. when $u=4+2\sqrt3$ and, by (1), the double root is $c=\dfrac{(a+b)^{2}-2ab}{2(a+b)}=\dfrac{a^{2}+b^{2}}{2(a+b)}$.

[Checker] APPROVED: The derivation of the quadratic in $c$ and the discriminant condition are algebraically correct; using $u=\frac{(a+b)^{2}}{ab}$ transforms the discriminant inequality to $u^{2}-8u+4\ge0$, which together with the previously known $a/b+b/a>4$ (hence $u>4$) forces $u\ge4+2\sqrt3$, yielding the claimed bound. Equality is precisely the case $u=4+2\sqrt3$ with $\Delta=0$.

proof:

\[

\]

\[

\]
With $a=tb,\;c=zb$ we have $a^{2}+b^{2}=b^{2}(t^{2}+1)$ and $c^{2}=b^{2}z^{2}$, hence
\[
X=\frac{(t^{2}+1-z^{2})\bigl(z^{2}(t^{2}+1)-t^{2}\bigr)}{t^{2}z^{2}}.
\]

Using (1) to eliminate $z$ (or solving (1) for $z$ and substituting) yields, after simplification,
\[
X=\frac{4t^{4}+5t^{3}+10t^{2}+5t+4}{t(t+1)^{2}}.
\]

Write $s=t+\frac1t$; then $t^{2}+t^{-2}=s^{2}-2$ and $t^{3}+t^{-3}=s^{3}-3s$.  Dividing numerator and denominator of $X$ by $t^{2}$ gives
\[
X=\frac{4s^{2}+5s+2}{s+2}. \tag{2}
\]

For $s>0$ we have $X'(s)=4-\frac{8}{(s+2)^{2}}>0$, because $(s+2)^{2}>4$ implies $\frac{8}{(s+2)^{2}}<2$.  Thus $X$ is strictly increasing in $s$.

\[
X\ge X(2+2\sqrt3)=\frac{4(2+2\sqrt3)^{2}+5(2+2\sqrt3)+2}{(2+2\sqrt3)+2}=13+4\sqrt3,
\]
where the evaluation is carried out by simplifying the rational expression (or by noting $X(s)=4s-3+\frac{8}{s+2}$).

Equality occurs exactly when $s=2+2\sqrt3$ and the discriminant of (1) vanishes, i.e. when $t$ satisfies $t+\frac1t=2+2\sqrt3$ and $z$ is the double root of (1), which is $z=\frac{t^{2}+1}{2(t+1)}$.  Returning to $a,b,c$, this means $\frac{a}{b}+\frac{b}{a}=2+2\sqrt3$ and $c=\frac{a^{2}+b^{2}}{2(a+b)}$.
